Derivative Applications and Integrals

Derivative Properties Summary

Def: the tangent to \(f(x)\) at \(x=a\) is equal to $$\lim_{x\to a} \frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a} = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} = f'(a)$$

Def: \(f(x)\) is diffrentiable at \(x=a\) if \(f'(a)\) exists

Properties~

$$diffrentiability \implies continuity$$

$$\frac{d}{dx}(f(x)+g(x))=f'(x)+g'(x)$$

$$\frac{d}{dx}(kf(x))=kf'(x)$$

$$\frac{d}{dx}(c)=0$$

$$\frac{d}{dx}(x^n)=nx^{n-1}$$

$$\frac{d}{dx}(\sin{x})=\cos{x}$$

$$\frac{d}{dx}(\cos{x})=-\sin{x}$$

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{1}{f(x)}\right)=-\frac{f'(x)}{(f(x))^2}$$

$$\frac{d}{dx}(f(x)g(x))=f'(x)g(x)+g'(x)f(x)$$

$$\frac{d}{dx}(f(g(x)))=f'(g(x))g'(x)$$

Proof Generalizations~ if you need to prove any of the derivative properties using limits,

1) convert definition to any of the limit definitions listed above

2) multiply to cancel out denominators or apply synthetic division

3) factor and cancel out numerator and denominator, or expand and cancel out h (depending on which definition you use)

Theorem: if \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) and diffrentiable on \((a,b)\) and \(f(a)=f(b)\), then \(\exists \ c \in (a,b) \ s.t. f'(c)=0\)

Proof: Case 1 $$f(a)=f(b)=max\ of\ f\ on\ [a,b]$$ $$\exists\ min \in [a,b] = c \implies f'(c)=0 $$ Case 2 $$f(a)=f(b)=min$$ $$\exists\ max \in [a,b] = c \implies f'(c)=0 $$ Case 3 $$f(a)=f(b)=min\land max$$ $$\forall c \in [a,b], f'(c)=0 $$

Theorem: if \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) and diffrentiable on \((a,b)\), then \(\exists \ c \in (a,b) \ s.t. f'(c)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\)

Proof: $$Let\ g(x)= f(x)-f(a)-\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}(x-a)$$ $$\implies g(a)=0\land g(b)=0$$ $$\implies \exists a < c < b\ s.t.\ g'(c)=0 $$ $$g'(x)=f'(x)-\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$$ $$\implies g'(c)=f'(c)-\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$$ $$\implies 0=f'(c)-\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a} $$ $$\implies f'(c)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$$

Theorem: suppose \(f\) and \(g\) are continuous on \([a,b]\) and diffrentiable on \((a,b)\), then \(\exists a < c < b\ s.t.\ \frac{g(b)-g(a)}{f(b)-f(a)}=\frac{g'(c)}{f'(c)}\)

Proof: The proof is very similar to the proof of the Mean Value Theorem in the sense that you create this function \(h(x)=(f(b)-f(a))g(x)-(g(b)-g(a)f(x))\), and the function is created such that \(h(a)=h(b)\) which lets you apply Rolle's Theorem, and you diffrentiate \(h(x)\), plug in c and \(h'(c)=0\) and simplify to the statement of Cauchy Mean Value Theorem.

Convexity

Def: A function \(f(x)\) is convex on \((a,b)\) if given \(a \leq r < s \leq b\), the secant line connecting \((r,f(r))\) to \((s,f(s))\) lies over the graph

Def: A function \(f(x)\) is concave on \((a,b)\) if given \(a \leq r < s \leq b\), the secant line connecting \((r,f(r))\) to \((s,f(s))\) lies under the graph (think mancave)

To derive symbolic definitions, draw a generic parabola and points a,b,\((c,f(c))\) inbetween the two points \((r,f(r))\) and \((s,f(s))\), then show that a generic point on the secant line \((c,?)\) is greater than the other point \((c,f(c))\). To prove any theorems concerning convexity, convert the convex definition into alternative form, and use the assumption and some generic points that utilize the assumption to eventually show the alternative form of the convex definition.

Derivative Tests

Def: First Derivative Test states that \(\forall a < c < b\ , f'(a) < 0 \land f'(b) > 0 \implies f(c)\) is a local min and \(\forall a < c < b\ , f'(a) > 0 \land f'(b) < 0 \implies f(c)\) is a local max

Def: Second Derivative Test states that \(f''(c) > 0 \implies f(c)\) is a local min and \(f''(c) < 0 \implies f(c)\) is a local max

Proof~

\(f''(c) > 0 \implies f'\) is increasing in a neighborhood of c

\(\implies f'\) goes from negative to positive

\(\implies f(c)\) is a local min by the First Derivative Test

Optimization Problems

The strategy to solving problems is

0) Draw,

1) Given,

2) Want (minimize or maximize some expression),

3) Relate,

4) Diffrentiate (to find critical points),

5) Check (plug in endpoints, critical points, and find what you want, apply FDT or SDT on answer and confirm it is a min or max),

6) State answer (written answer with units)

Parametrics and Implicit Diffrentiation

Recall~ sometimes it is convenient to write \(x\) and \(y\) in terms of another variable, called a parameter (normally denoted \(t\))

$$\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{\frac{dy}{dt}}{\frac{dx}{dt}}$$

this is true because we can cancel out denominators like fractions

Sample Problem~ Find the equation of the normal (a line perpendicular) to the 4-petal rose, \(r=\sin 2\theta\) when \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{3}\)

Generalized Solution ~ Find the slope of the line, i.e. \(\frac{dy}{dx}\), by first converting the polar equation to \(x=\) (something in terms of \(\theta\)) and \(y=\) (something in terms of \(\theta\)), utilizing the property from before \(\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{\frac{dy}{dt}}{\frac{dx}{dt}}\), find a point that lies on the normal by plugging \(\theta\) into the equation and solving for r, use point-slope formula to derive equation of the normal

Def: implicit diffrentiation is simply taking the derivative of both sides of an equation that relate one variable to another

Example~ an equation that relates x and y would be \(x^2+y^2=1\) and implicit diffrentiation would be \(\frac{d}{dx}(x^2+y^2)=\frac{d}{dx}(1)\)

Inverse Function Applications

Def: a function \(f(x)\) is monotonic on an interval if it is either increasing or decreasing on that interval

Theorem: If \(f(x)\) is increasing then so is \(f^{-1}(x)\)

Proof: $$(f(x)\ is\ increasing) \implies (x < y \implies f(x) < f(y)) $$ $$f(x)\geq f(y) \implies x \geq y $$ $$Let\ (f(x)=\tilde{x})\land (f(y)=\tilde{y}) $$ $$\implies (x=f^{-1}(\tilde{x})))\land (y=f^{-1}(\tilde{y}))$$ $$\tilde{x} \geq \tilde{y} \implies f^{-1}(\tilde{x}) \geq f^{-1}(\tilde{y})$$ $$\tilde{x} > \tilde{y} \implies f^{-1}(\tilde{x}) > f^{-1}(\tilde{y})$$ $$f^{-1}(x)\ is\ increasing$$

Theorem: If \(f(x)\) is continuous and increasing on \([a,b]\) then \(f^{-1}(x)\) is continuous on \([f(a),f(b)]\)

Proof: Convert \(f^{-1}(x)\) is continuous on \([f(a),f(b)]\) to \(\epsilon,\delta\) definition. Take the \(f^{-1}\) of the expanded version of the hypothesis of the definition. Take the new conditional statement, and convert into something \(\subseteq\) of another thing (by subset and conditional statement properties). Solve for \(\delta\) in the statement. State \(\delta \leq min\) of something and generate formal proof.

Theorem: Assume \(f(x)\) is continuous and diffrentiable and monotonic, then \((f^{-1})'(b)=\frac{1}{f'(f^{-1}(b))}\)

Proof: $$Let\ (a,f(a))\ be\ a\ point\ on\ f(x)$$ $$\implies (f(a),a)\ is\ a\ point\ on\ f'(x)$$ $$\implies (b,f^{-1}(b))\ is\ a\ point\ on\ f'(x)$$ $$\implies f'(x)\ slope = (f^{-1})'(b) = \frac{1}{f(x)\ slope}=\frac{1}{f'(a)}=\frac{1}{f'(f^{-1}(b))}$$ $$\implies (f^{-1})'(b)=\frac{1}{f'(f^{-1}(b))}$$

Theorem: Assume \(f\) is increasing and diffrentiable on \([a,b]\), then \(f^{-1}\) is diffrentiable at \(f(c)=d\) and \(f'(c) \neq 0\)

Proof: Convert \((f^{-1})'(d)\) to h limit definition. Plug in alternative values for \(f^{-1}(d+h)\) , \(f^{-1}(d)\) and \(h\). Convert \(\lim_{h\to 0}\) into \(\lim_{\tilde{h}\to 0}\). Simplify, show diffrentiability exists.

Inverse Trig Functions

To find the domains and ranges of inverse trig functions, simply use the understanding of what part of the normal trig function is used to create the inverse trig function, then take the domain and range of the normal function for that piece, flip it, and use as the domains and ranges of inverse trig. (remember normal \(\sin\) passes through origin while normal \(\cos\) does not)

\(\sin^{-1}\)~ take the piece starting from \(-\frac{\pi}{2}\) to \(\frac{\pi}{2}\) (i.e. valley to peak)

\(\cos^{-1}\)~ take the piece starting from \(0\) to \(\pi\) (i.e. peak to valley)

\(\tan^{-1}\)~ take the piece starting from \(-\frac{\pi}{2}\) to \(\frac{\pi}{2}\) (i.e. one repeating part only)

To find an equation for the derivative of the inverse trig functions (such as \((\sin^{-1})'\)), set \(y =\ inverse\ trig\ function\), solve for \(x\), take \(\frac{d}{dx}\) of both sides, solve for \(\frac{dy}{dx}\), plug in \(y =\ inverse\ trig\ function\), simplify. Or you can simply use the formula derived before~ \((f^{-1})'(b)=\frac{1}{f'(f^{-1}(b))}\), and to simplify inverse trig, let \(\theta = \sin^{-1}(x) \implies x=\sin(\theta)\), then we have a triangle with hypotenuse length 1, side length \(x\) for the side across from \(\theta\) and \(\sqrt{1-x^2}\), and to find something like \(\cos(\sin^{-1}(x))\) it is simply \(\cos{\theta}\) which equals \(\frac{\sqrt{1-x^2}}{1}\).
